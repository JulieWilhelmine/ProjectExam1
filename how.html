How do you become an astronaut?

Requirements

Being an astronaut is a real job and real people, like you, can apply for it! You can safely say though that there is no other job like it, and it's the only one that might take you off this very planet and back. Most of an astronauts time isn't spent in space though, but on Johnson's Space Centre in Houston, Texas, if you're hired by NASA. The day's won't ever be the same and you might be flying a jet one day, checking on supplies for the ISS the next, do speeches in elementary schools to get students exited about engineering and science, or spend the day 40 feet underwater in a heavy suit – testing things for future spacewalks.

It is an amazing and unique job, but it is hard to get in. The odds are slighly above one-in-a-thousand, meaning that if NASA get's 6 000 applicants, they maybe hire 8 of them.

As for pure requirements you need at least a bachelors degree in Engineering, Physical Science, Computer Science, Biological Science or Mathemathics. So if you have a passion for either of those fields of study, taking that route might get you closer to your astronaut dreams! Physical health is also important and to become an astronaut you need good eyesight, blood pressure within the normal range and to be in general good physical condition – so staying healthy is key! You can become an astronaut in many countries, but speaking english is a requirement, as the space station is international.

Training

As you can imagine, becoming an astronnaut requires a lot of training, and it can take up to 2 years to become fully qualified. Many hours will be spent flying training jets and learning the basics of the space shuttle and station. Since giving speeches is a big part of the job, public speaking classes is an important part of the training as well. There's also language classes to take, especially since the astronauts must be able to communicate with the Russian Mission Control Centre.

Being in space can be very dangerous, so medical and survival training is very important so the astronauts are well prepared in case of emergency. There are life sized models of the space station where practice takes place – a smart way to test the conditions they're going to work in before actually going to space, since floating around in microgravity for example can be a bit hard to imagine before having tried it. A lot of training time is actually spent under water, as the change in gravity that portrays, as well as the pressures and lack of air can be a good way to simulate a space environment.
